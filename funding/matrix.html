Functional Architecture of Operational Governance for Threshold Dynamics

Research Purpose

This review matrix is designed to test whether existing literature provides an operational, cross-sectoral governance framework capable of observing, interpreting, and acting upon threshold dynamics before systemic conditions become materially irreversible.

RQ1: What functions must an operational, cross-sectoral governance framework perform in order to act on threshold dynamics before irreversibility, according to the existing literature?

RQ2: To what extent do existing frameworks individually satisfy those functions?

Preliminary Interpretation

The preliminary screening suggests a fragmentation pattern: financial macroprudential frameworks are operational but sector-specific, while cross-sectoral and threshold-oriented frameworks remain weaker on executable decision protocols and reversibility.

Among the corpus, only one entry is classified as an operational system. Coverage claims should therefore be reported within type classes rather than across the full set.

Codebook
Seven Functional Criteria

Codebook

Scoring rubric for the preliminary screening matrix
Function Definition Score 3 Standard
F1 — Operational Architecture Design of indicators, roles, processes, and institutional architecture. Indicators, roles, workflow, and governance process are explicitly defined.
F2 — Decision Protocol Executable trigger logic: who acts, when, and under what criterion. Decision triggers, actors, thresholds, and actions are operationally specified.
F3 — Cross-Sector Integration Ability to operate across three or more interdependent sectors. Framework applies to multiple interacting sectors simultaneously.
F4 — Threshold Dynamics Dynamic treatment of proximity, velocity, uncertainty, or transition risk. Threshold movement or approach is modeled dynamically, not statically.
F5 — Observability Leading indicators and pre-event detection capacity. Indicators detect meaningful pre-event signals before failure materializes.
F6 — Ex-Ante Governance Governance action before the critical point or irreversible transition. Framework defines preventive action before collapse or irreversible damage.
F7 — Reversibility Treatment of reversibility window as an explicit decision variable. Framework explicitly governs action while conditions remain reversible.

The seven functions are treated as provisional outputs of the screening protocol. They are not presented as c-ECO assumptions. Their final status depends on RQ1 coding, full-text verification, and synthesis across the expanded corpus.

Analytical Results

Interpretive findings from the screening-stage corpus

These findings are preliminary patterns, not final conclusions. They are subject to revision after source verification, expanded corpus coding, and full PRISMA-ScR execution.

Pattern 1 — Functional Fragmentation

The screening corpus suggests that the literature contains strong functional components, but these components are distributed across different traditions rather than integrated into a single operational framework.

Financial macroprudential frameworks tend to score strongly on operational architecture, decision protocols, observability, and ex-ante governance, but remain sector-specific.

Earth-system, resilience, cascading-failure, and polycrisis frameworks tend to score more strongly on cross-sector integration and threshold dynamics, but remain weaker on executable decision protocols and reversibility.

Pattern 2 — Decision Protocol Deficit
Function Distribution Highlight Interpretive Reading
F2 — Decision Protocol 32 entries score 1; only 1 entry scores 3. Most frameworks mention decision or governance but do not define executable trigger activation.
F7 — Reversibility 26 entries score 1; only 1 entry scores 3. Reversibility is usually conceptual, not operationalized as a decision variable.
