International Statute on Systemic Governance

The c-ECO Systemic Governance Statute establishes that the validity, efficacy, and continuity of legal and economic relations depend upon the material conditions that sustain life, economic activity, and the functional integrity of the Earth System.

Contemporary legal systems have incorporated anticipatory instruments — precautionary principles, due diligence obligations, risk-based regulation, and macroprudential supervision. Yet these instruments remain sectorally fragmented and do not condition enforceability upon continuously measured and verifiable systemic states.

Under conditions of cumulative effects, feedback dynamics, and non-linear propagation, the window for effective intervention may close prior to the manifestation of legally cognizable damage. The Doctrine introduces the concept of Trajectory Illegality: formally lawful acts may become legally incompatible with systemic stability when their technically foreseeable continuation leads toward ecosystem degradation or critical infrastructure disruption.

Core Legal Innovations

Pre-Threshold Responsibility

The duty to act when systemic trajectory is detectably constrained but before formal breach occurs. A juridical category that closes the recognition-action gap.

Threshold Function Protocol

A technical-legal instrument that converts monitoring signals into governance triggers, activating Safe Mode when certified systemic conditions are met.

Trajectory Illegality

The principle that formally valid acts may become legally incompatible when their continuation leads toward irreversible systemic degradation.

Safe Operating Space

A conditioned governance environment in which legal and economic operations are bounded by continuously verified systemic states.
