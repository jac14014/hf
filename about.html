Foundation Origin

From the Amazon
to Systemic Governance

The Johann Christian Hasse Foundation was not born in a library. It was born from 22 years of running businesses in Belém do Pará — where contracts failed, supply chains collapsed, and systemic risk was not a concept but a daily reality.

The Story Behind the Framework

1993 — 2015 — Entrepreneur in the Amazon. Jacqueline A. Ennis built and operated the African Bar (1993–2005) and the Palafita Bar (2005–2015) in Belém do Pará. For 22 years, she managed contracts and supply chains in one of Brazil's most complex environmental contexts, learning firsthand how contracts break when systemic conditions shift.

2016 — 2021 — From Brazil to Boston. Paralegal Studies at Boston University, working as Contract Administrator at Bluebird Network, observing the same patterns: contracts designed for stability that become traps when external conditions change.

2021 — 2023 — LL.M. in Business Law at Washington University in St. Louis. Formalization of the Recognition-Action Gap: legal systems recognize harm only after it materializes. There is no juridical category for pre-threshold responsibility.

2023 — 2024 — COP30 and the Birth of the Doctrine. When Belém was selected to host COP30, the c-ECO framework crystallized as a legal architecture for anticipatory governance.

Institutional Lineage

The Foundation traces its juridical lineage to Johann Christian Hasse (1779–1830), a German jurist whose work on institutional continuity and legal memory informs the Foundation's archival mission. The name carries a commitment to scholarship that outlives its authors.

Established in February 2026, the Foundation operates as an institution of record: quiet, researched, legally literate, and built for longevity. We preserve institutional memory as a public asset, develop legal frameworks for systemic and ecological risk, and translate archival continuity into research, doctrine, and field action.
